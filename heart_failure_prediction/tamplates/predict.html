Blood Pressure

The force of circulating blood on the walls of the arteries or blood pressure is the measurement of pressure of the blood in your arteries, which are vessels that carry your blood from your heart to the rest of your body. Blood pressure is taken using two measurements: systolic (measured when the heart beats, when blood pressure is at its highest) and diastolic (measured between heart beats, when blood pressure is at its lowest). Blood pressure is written with the systolic blood pressure first, followed by the diastolic blood pressure (for example 120/80).

Cholesterol

Cholesterol is a waxy, fat-like substance that's found in all the cells in your body. Your body needs some cholesterol to work properly. But if you have too much of it in your blood, it can stick to the walls of your arteries and narrow or even block them. This puts you at risk of coronary artery disease and other heart diseases.

Blood Sugar

Blood sugar level, also known as blood glucose level, refers to the amount of glucose, a type of sugar, in your blood. Glucose is your body's primary source of energy, derived from the food you eat. Blood sugar levels fluctuate throughout the day and are generally higher after eating and lower when fasting. Normal blood sugar levels generally range from 4.0 to 7.8 mmol/L (72 to 140 mg/dL) for individuals without diabetes.

Heart Rate

The heart rate measures the number of times per minute that the heart contracts or beats. Most adults have a target resting heart rate of 60 to 100 beats per minute. A heart rate exceeding 100 bpm at rest is considered high.

Chest Pain
  1. Typical Angina (TA)
    • What it is: A predictable chest pain caused by physical activity or stress. It goes away with rest or medicine (like nitroglycerin).
    • Example: You walk up stairs and feel chest tightness, but it goes away after sitting down.
  2. Atypical Angina (ATA)
    • What it is: Chest pain that is less predictable. It might not be caused by exercise or may not go away with rest.
    • Example: You feel chest discomfort while sitting or after eating, not when you're active.
  3. Non-Anginal Pain (NAP)
    • What it is: Chest pain not related to the heart. Could be due to gas, muscle strain, acid reflux, etc.
    • Example: Sharp chest pain when you move or twist your body.
  4. Asymptomatic (ASY)
    • What it is: No chest pain at all, even if there's heart disease. This is common in elderly or diabetic patients.
    • Example: A heart problem is found during a test, but the person never felt chest pain.
